Introduction to Melanoma

Melanoma is a serious form of skin cancer that develops from melanocytes, the cells responsible for producing melanin, which gives skin its color. While it accounts for a small percentage of skin cancer cases, melanoma is known for its aggressive nature and ability to spread to other parts of the body if not detected early.

Key Points:

  • Causes: The primary risk factors for melanoma include excessive exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun or tanning beds, having fair skin, a history of sunburns, and family history of skin cancer.
  • Symptoms: Melanoma can appear as a new or changing mole. Key warning signs include asymmetry, irregular borders, multiple colors, a diameter larger than 6mm, and any changes in size, shape, or color of an existing mole (often referred to as the "ABCDE" rule).
  • Diagnosis: Early detection is crucial. A dermatologist can perform a visual examination and may recommend a biopsy to confirm a diagnosis.
  • Treatment: Options for treating melanoma may include surgical removal of the tumor, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, chemotherapy, and radiation therapy, depending on the stage and characteristics of the cancer.

Awareness and education about melanoma are vital for early detection and effective treatment, making it essential for individuals to monitor their skin and consult healthcare professionals when changes occur.
